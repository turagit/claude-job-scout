# Psychology Cheatsheet for CV Writing

Quick-reference for evidence-based persuasion techniques. Apply these when writing or reviewing CV content.

## Cognitive Biases That Influence Hiring Decisions

### 1. Anchoring Effect
**What:** The first piece of information sets the reference point for all subsequent judgments.
**CV Application:** Lead each role section with your most impressive metric. If a recruiter sees "Grew ARR from £1.2M to £5.8M" first, every subsequent bullet is evaluated against that strong anchor.
**How to apply:** Reorder bullets so the highest-impact achievement is always first under each role.

### 2. Peak-End Rule (Kahneman)
**What:** People judge an experience based on its peak moment and its ending, not the average.
**CV Application:** The "peak" is your strongest single bullet across the whole CV. The "end" is whatever the reader sees last (usually Education or a closing section). Both must be strong.
**How to apply:** Ensure your single best achievement is visually prominent (first bullet of most recent role). Make the last section of the CV substantive — don't let it trail off with "Hobbies: reading."

### 3. Social Proof
**What:** People trust choices validated by others, especially recognizable others.
**CV Application:** Named clients (especially well-known brands), team sizes, user counts, and recognizable company names all trigger trust.
**How to apply:** Replace vague references with specific ones: "for a major bank" → "for Barclays." Mention team sizes, user counts, and revenue figures. Use "Selected by...", "Recommended by...", "Trusted by..." where true.

### 4. Specificity Bias
**What:** Specific claims feel more credible and researched than round numbers.
**CV Application:** "Reduced page load time by 37%" feels more believable and precise than "Reduced page load time by about 40%."
**How to apply:** Use exact numbers wherever honestly possible. Even adding a decimal ("3.2x improvement") increases perceived credibility.

### 5. Contrast Effect
**What:** Items are judged relative to what's adjacent, not in absolute terms.
**CV Application:** A strong metric placed right after a role title (which sets a neutral baseline) creates a positive contrast. Grouping multiple strong bullets together diminishes each one.
**How to apply:** Interleave strong-metric bullets with context bullets so each strong one pops.

### 6. Loss Aversion (Kahneman & Tversky)
**What:** Losses feel ~2x more painful than equivalent gains feel good.
**CV Application:** Framing achievements as losses prevented can be more compelling than gains achieved.
**How to apply:** "Prevented £2M revenue loss by identifying critical payment bug" > "Saved £2M by fixing a payment bug." Use "prevented", "protected", "retained", "de-risked" for defensive achievements.

### 7. Authority Heuristic
**What:** People defer to perceived experts and authority figures.
**CV Application:** Certifications, publications, conference talks, advisory roles, and selection for special initiatives all signal authority.
**How to apply:** Don't bury credentials. Place certifications near the top (in summary or a dedicated section). Use phrases like "selected for", "appointed to", "invited to" — these imply external validation.

### 8. Halo Effect
**What:** Positive impression in one area colors perception of everything else.
**CV Application:** One impressive company name or achievement creates a positive halo over the entire CV.
**How to apply:** If you've worked at a recognizable brand (FAANG, Big 4, FTSE-100), make sure it's visible in the first scan. Lead your summary with your most prestigious affiliation if relevant.

### 9. Scarcity & Exclusivity
**What:** Rare things are perceived as more valuable.
**CV Application:** "One of 3 engineers selected for the founding team" is more compelling than "Joined the team." Rare skill combinations, niche expertise, or competitive selections all trigger this.
**How to apply:** Highlight selectivity: "Chosen from 200 applicants", "One of 5 certified practitioners in the UK", "Invited to join..."

### 10. Narrative Transportation
**What:** People are more persuaded by coherent stories than by disconnected facts.
**CV Application:** A CV that tells a clear career progression story (specialization deepening, scope expanding, impact growing) is more memorable than a list of disconnected roles.
**How to apply:** The professional summary should frame the narrative. Each role should show progression. If the story isn't linear, the summary must explain the thread.

## The 7-Second Scan Psychology

Research shows recruiters spend an average of 6–7 seconds on an initial CV scan. During this time, eye-tracking studies show they follow an **F-pattern**:

1. **Horizontal scan across the top** — name, current title, summary
2. **Horizontal scan of first role** — company, title, first 1–2 bullets
3. **Vertical scan down the left side** — company names, role titles, dates

**Implication:** The top-left quadrant of page 1 is the highest-value real estate. Place your name, a compelling summary, your most recent/impressive role, and your strongest achievement there.

## Applying Psychology Without Manipulation

These techniques work because they align the CV's presentation with how human cognition naturally processes information. The goal is **clarity and memorability**, not deception:

- Never fabricate achievements or inflate numbers
- Never claim credentials you don't have
- Use specificity bias only with real numbers
- Frame truthful achievements using the most compelling angle
- The best CV is one where every claim can be defended in an interview
